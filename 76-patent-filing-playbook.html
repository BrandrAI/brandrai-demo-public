1 · Why two patents

Narrow + broad. Both. Same inventors.

Your existing provisional covers the cannabis-specific COA verification pipeline. It claims a particular application: lab documents for regulated commerce, tied to Metrc/BioTrack integration, scored against known-lab databases. That's defensible and specific — ideal for defending against another cannabis-tech company copying your exact product.

BrandrAI Proof is a general-purpose claim. It covers the underlying method — timestamp + AI metadata extraction + forensic scoring + public verify page — applied to any digital content, not just lab docs. That claim is broader, harder to invalidate, and harder for a competitor to work around.

You want both because they defend different things:

Cannabis patent (existing)
Narrow + deep

  • Hard to invent around for the cannabis use case
  • Tied to Metrc/BioTrack + consumer verify pages

Proof primitive (new)

If you only file the narrow one, a competitor builds the broad engine and licenses it to non-cannabis verticals. If you only file the broad one, a copycat builds the cannabis product and argues their use was obvious given state-compliance data. You need both.

2 · Anatomy of a provisional

What the USPTO actually wants.

A provisional patent application locks in a priority date for 12 months. You have exactly one year to convert it to a non-provisional or lose the priority date. It's not examined on the merits — the USPTO just confirms the paperwork is complete and stamps the date.

What a good provisional contains:

Section Purpose Who writes it
TitleOne-line name of the inventionAttorney + you
Field of inventionWhat technical space this is inAttorney
BackgroundState of the art + the problem your invention solvesAttorney + you
SummaryPlain-language overview of what you builtYou
Detailed descriptionDeep technical walkthrough of every step, component, and variationYou + Dang
Drawings / figuresFlowcharts, system diagrams, data structuresYou + Dang
Claims (optional in provisional)Legal scope of protection — but not required until non-provisionalAttorney
Inventor declaration + feeNames, addresses, signatures, $320 USPTO fee (small entity)Attorney files

Key principle: the provisional's value depends entirely on how much technical detail it contains. Skimpy provisional = weak priority date because it doesn't describe enough to "teach" the invention. Rich provisional = strong priority date that survives examination of the non-provisional a year later.

3 · Cannabis patent

What you already have + what's missing.

You filed a provisional on the cannabis COA verification pipeline earlier this year. Before the 12-month conversion deadline, you want to:

  1. Audit the current provisional. Pull the filing from your counsel, review what's actually claimed vs. what you've built since. Cannabis product has evolved (Metrc state-specific routing, PackageVerification refactor, Pusher real-time, THC product type, forensic scoring rubric). Much of that is beyond the original provisional.
  2. File a continuation-in-part (CIP) or a second provisional. CIP adds new matter to the existing case while preserving the original priority date for the unchanged parts. Second provisional starts a fresh priority date for the new material. Counsel decides which path based on how much has changed.
  3. Convert the original to a non-provisional before the 12-month deadline. This is the step where the claims get formalized. Non-provisional application fee: $1,600-$3,200 for small entity + $8-15K in attorney time.
What to add in the CIP or second provisional
  • Metrc state-specific URL routing mechanism (the matrix that maps license → state → endpoint)
  • PackageVerification unified data model (abstracting `source=metrc|upload` into one table)
  • Per-product-type AI prompt selection (how the system picks the THC vs CBD vs peptide extraction template)
  • Forensic scoring rubric — the specific 3-axis scoring (Document Structure / Data Integrity / Red Flag) with defined weights
  • Live broadcasting of verification state via server-sent events or WebSocket (Pusher integration)
  • Pairing of cryptographic hash with AI-extracted metadata in a single bound certificate (claims this combination as novel in the cannabis space)
Important: anything you've publicly disclosed (pitch decks, investor conversations, demo event) potentially starts the 1-year clock for the non-provisional conversion. USPTO grants a 12-month grace period in the US, but foreign filings (EU, Madrid) have no grace period. File before May 1 launch if you want international options.
4 · Proof primitive patent

Brand-new provisional. File before May 1 too.

This is the general-purpose claim. It covers the combined pipeline of:

  1. Receiving arbitrary digital content (document, image, video, dataset, source file)
  2. Computing a cryptographic hash of that content bound to a trusted UTC timestamp
  3. Using a large language model to extract structured metadata from the content
  4. Running deterministic forensic authenticity scoring against domain-specific rules
  5. Producing a tamper-evident public verify page addressed by a UUID and optionally encoded in a QR code
  6. Exporting a signed PDF certificate suitable for legal attestation

The novelty is in the combination. Timestamping exists. AI metadata extraction exists. Tamper-evident hashing exists. Nobody has combined them into a single attestation pipeline that outputs a court-admissible certificate AND a consumer-accessible verify page. That bundle is the patentable invention.

What the technical disclosure has to describe
  • The architecture diagram (user upload → hash + timestamp → AI extraction → forensic scoring → certificate + verify page)
  • The specific deterministic scoring function (weights, thresholds, verdict logic) — described generically, not cannabis-specific
  • How product-type or domain-type routing selects which AI prompt + scoring rules apply
  • How the hash is re-verified on the public page so the proof is decentralized in trust
  • Optional: on-chain anchoring as one embodiment; server-only as another; hybrid as a third
  • The UUID-to-content binding mechanism that prevents certificate forgery
  • Variations — documents, images, video, datasets, software releases — each as a separate embodiment

Co-inventors: Max + Dang at minimum. Anyone else who contributed a non-obvious technical insight should be listed. Leaving someone off later is grounds for invalidation. Better to err on the side of inclusion and assign rights via a stock-linked IP assignment agreement (which Clerky's post-incorporation package handles).

6 · Picking counsel

Not every patent attorney is right for this.

You need a firm or solo practitioner with specific qualifications:

Must-haves

  • USPTO-registered patent agent or patent attorney (not just any IP lawyer)
  • Computer science / software engineering technical background — this is a software method patent
  • Experience with AI / LLM-related patents specifically (rapidly evolving area)
  • Willing to work on fixed-fee basis for provisional stage (avoid billable-hour surprises)
  • Comfortable with cannabis-adjacent IP (not all firms take this work)

Red flags

  • Quotes $1,500 for a provisional — too cheap, means cookie-cutter boilerplate
  • No software / SaaS patents in their portfolio
  • Pressures you to file quickly without doing prior-art search
  • Won't share redacted samples of prior provisionals they've drafted
  • Refuses to put fee structure in writing before starting work
Where to find good ones
  • Your network: Sheri + the '68 Partners advisor probably have 2-3 referrals each. Warm intros beat cold outreach 10:1.
  • Clerky's network: since you're using Clerky for incorporation, ask Brandon Nguyen for patent-attorney recommendations from their vetted list.
  • Acquirer's alumni networks: former USPTO examiners who moved into private practice often know the craft better than big-firm associates.
  • Boutique IP firms: search “software patent attorney small entity provisional” — target firms with 2-20 attorneys, not big firms with 200+.
7 · Your inputs

What you and Dang have to produce.

The attorney writes the legal language. You write the technical substance. Here's what you hand over.

1. Engineering disclosure document (per patent)

A 10-20 page technical write-up describing every material component of the invention. I'm drafting both of these as separate files (74-patent-disclosure-cannabis.html, 75-patent-disclosure-proof.html) for you to review and hand to counsel.

  • What problem it solves
  • How it works, step by step, at technical depth
  • What variations exist (alternative embodiments)
  • What specific data structures, algorithms, and integrations are used
  • What's different from the prior art you're aware of

2. Flow diagrams + system architecture

Visual representations of how data moves through the system. Boxes and arrows. USPTO loves figures.

  • High-level system architecture (services, data stores, external dependencies)
  • Data flow from upload → hash → AI → score → certificate
  • State machine for verification status (pending/processing/completed/failed)
  • Entity relationship for PackageVerification model

3. Inventor declaration

Signed statement from each inventor: full legal name, address, citizenship, relationship to the company. Max is US citizen. Dang's Vietnamese citizenship is fine — inventors don't need to be US citizens; applications just need a US filing agent (your attorney).

4. Assignment agreements

IP from the inventors has to be legally assigned to BrandrAI, Inc. Clerky's post-incorporation package generates these as part of founder stock issuance + CIIAs. Do this BEFORE filing — inventions must be owned by the entity that files the patent, not by the individual inventors.

5. Prior-art search (attorney produces, you review)

Attorney pulls existing patents, academic papers, and public product disclosures that overlap with your claims. You review for accuracy. This protects against wasted filing on an invention someone already has.

8 · Common mistakes

What founders get wrong.

1
Filing provisional without a real disclosure. A weak provisional that just says “a system for verifying documents” won't support the non-provisional claims 12 months later. The priority date doesn't stretch to cover stuff that wasn't described. Fix: write a thorough technical disclosure now.
2
Missing foreign filing deadlines. US has a 1-year grace period for public disclosure. EU and Madrid Protocol don't — any public disclosure before filing in those jurisdictions kills the claim. Fix: file provisionals BEFORE May 1 launch event.
3
Not assigning rights to the company first. If Max and Dang own inventions personally but the patent application is filed in BrandrAI, Inc.'s name, that's a technical defect. Fix: sign CIIAs through Clerky before filing; attach the assignment cover sheet.
4
Forgetting about co-inventors. If a contractor, advisor, or early hire contributed a non-obvious technical step, they're an inventor by law. Leaving them off is grounds for invalidation. Fix: audit every significant design meeting and code author; list everyone who qualifies.
5
Telling too much to investors without NDAs. Every investor conversation before filing is a potential public disclosure. Fix: NDA in place for anyone seeing the engine-level detail (use 68-investor-nda-flow.html).
6
Writing claims in the provisional that are too narrow. Counterintuitive: provisional has no claims required, but if you include sample claims, they frame how counsel thinks about the non-provisional. Fix: either include no claims in the provisional, or draft broad method claims with counsel review.
