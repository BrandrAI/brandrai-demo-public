What it is

A general-purpose authenticity engine. Any digital document — PDF, image, spreadsheet, dataset, source file — is hashed (SHA-256), bound to a trusted UTC timestamp, analyzed by an LLM for structured metadata, and issued as a signed PDF certificate plus a public QR-addressable verify page. The entire chain is tamper-evident: any alteration invalidates the proof.

Built originally for cannabis COA verification (live in production, Ohio launch May 1, 2026). The underlying engine is vertical-agnostic. We're now positioning it as a standalone product.

Why we win

The authenticity market exists, but fragmented into single primitives. Nobody has bundled the stack the way we have.

  • OpenTimestamps / blockchain timestamping: technically capable, developer-only UX, no certificate, no AI layer. Engineers only.
  • US Copyright Office / WGA: legally accepted, 3-month turnaround, one format, $20-65 each. Not operational.
  • DocuSign / Adobe Sign CLM: owns signing workflow, doesn't own tamper-evident layer. Partnership territory, not replacement.
  • Vanta / Drata / Secureframe: own SOC 2 workflow, use manual evidence. Per-document cryptographic evidence is our layer beneath them.
  • Stampery / WordProof / Surety: 2015-era crypto-first attempts, mostly dead. Led with tech; we lead with the use case.

Bundle advantage: cryptographic hash + trusted timestamp + AI metadata extraction + forensic authenticity scoring + court-ready certificate + public verify page. Nobody else has all six in one product.

Questions you'll ask, answered up front

  • “Is this actually admissible?” — Federal courts accept cryptographic hash + trusted timestamp under the self-authenticating provisions of FRE 902(13) and 902(14) for ESI. Burden is on the proponent to lay foundation; we make that straightforward with the certificate. We want your read on whether that's the right frame.
  • “21 CFR Part 11?” — Proof produces records meeting the Part 11 technical requirements for electronic signatures + audit trails. It is not a standalone Part 11 solution; it's the evidentiary layer inside a compliant workflow.
  • “HIPAA?” — Proof of a document's integrity doesn't require access to its contents. For PHI-containing documents, we hash in-browser before upload so we never receive PHI. That's the “BAA not required” architecture. Optional BAA available for enterprise tier.
  • “Can someone forge a certificate?” — No. The public verify page re-computes the hash server-side and compares. Any tampered certificate doesn't verify.
